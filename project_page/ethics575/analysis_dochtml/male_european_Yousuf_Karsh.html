    Yousuf Karsh, Who Photographed Famous And Infamous of 20th Century, Dies at 93

    1Yousuf Karsh , whose photographic portraits of the famous and the powerful are known to millions throughout the world , died yesterday at 93 .
    2He died after surgery at Brigham and Women 's Hospital in Boston , said his nephew , Sidney Karsh .
    3Best known for his World War II portraits of Winston Churchill , Dwight D. Eisenhower and other leaders of the Allied cause , Karsh of Ottawa , as he was called professionally , traveled all over the world to photograph political and military leaders , as well as celebrated writers , artists and entertainers .
    4His portraits were reproduced widely in newspapers and magazines as well as in books , and many of them have become the best loved and most familiar images of their subjects .
    5His most famous portraits include : the 1941 picture of Winston Churchill as an indomitable wartime leader ; George Bernard Shaw as quizzical old sage in 1943 ; Dwight D. Eisenhower in 1946 as a five-star general and Supreme Commander of the Allied Expeditionary Force ; Ernest Hemingway in a turtle-neck sweater in 1957 ; Georgia O'Keeffe in her New Mexico studio in 1956 and Nikita S. Khrushchev swathed in fur in 1963 .
    6He also photographed Harry S. Truman , John F. Kennedy , Fidel Castro and Andy Warhol .
    7Mr. Karsh was a master of the formally posed , carefully lighted studio portrait .
    8Working with an 8-by-10 view camera and a battery of artificial lights ( he was said to carry 350 pounds of equipment on his trips abroad ) he aimed , in his own words , '' to stir the emotions of the viewer '' and to '' lay bare the soul '' of his sitter .
    9He characteristically achieved a heroic monumentality in which the sitter 's face , grave , thoughtful and impressive , emerged from a dark , featureless background with an almost superhuman grandeur .
    10As the historian Peter Pollack put it in his '' Picture History of Photography '' , '' Yousuf Karsh , in his powerful portraits , transforms the human face into legend . ''
    11Yousuf Karsh was born of Armenian parents in Mardin , Turkey , on Dec. 23 , 1908 .
    12In 1924 he immigrated to Canada , where his uncle , a studio portrait photographer , taught him the basis of his profession .
    13After a three-year apprenticeship to John H. Garo of Boston , a well-known portrait photographer of the era and a fellow Armenian , Mr. Karsh settled in Ottawa and opened his own portrait studio in 1932 .
    14He learned the dramatic use of artificial light that became the hallmark of his mature portrait style by studying theatrical lighting at the Ottawa Little Theater , of which he was a member .
    15In Garo 's studio he had worked with natural light .
    16At the theater he met the son of Lord Bessborough , the governor general of Canada , who persuaded his father to sit for Mr. Karsh , initiating a long and close relationship between the photographer and successive Canadian political figures .
    17The next governor general , Lord Tweedsmuir , introduced him to Prime Minister Mackenzie King , who brought him in to photograph Winston Churchill during the latter 's wartime visit to Canada in December 1941 .
    18The Churchill portrait was a turning point in Mr. Karsh 's career .
    19He was given only two minutes to take it , during which he is said to have angered the visiting statesman by taking away his cigar before shooting .
    20However , the portrait that resulted , showing the British prime minister glowering at the camera with a bulldoglike tenaciousness , seemed to epitomize the determination of the British to defeat Hitler , and catapulted Mr. Karsh into international fame .
