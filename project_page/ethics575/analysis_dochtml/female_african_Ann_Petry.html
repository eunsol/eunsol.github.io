    Ann Petry, 88, First to Write A Literary Portrait of Harlem

    1Ann Petry , who took a single stretch of Harlem and brought it vividly and disturbingly to life in her acclaimed 1946 novel , '' The Street , '' died on Monday at a convalescent center near her home in Old Saybrook , Conn .
    2She was 88 .
    3So much has been written about the hard life on the the mean streets of Harlem , it may seem surprising that the first major literary work to focus on that life would be a first novel by a woman who not only had not grown up there but whose exposure to Harlem street culture had been limited to nine months working with an experimental after-school program on West 116th Street .
    4But then Ann Lane Petry was a surprising woman , one of such enormous empathy and imaginative flair that she did not need to encounter many bereft latchkey children , their desperate , beleaguered mothers or the assortment of opportunistic men who preyed on them to translate her observations into literature .
    5Half a century after '' The Street , '' was first published by Houghton-Mifflin , Mrs. Petry 's creations , Ludie Johnson and her 8-year-old son Bub , are still regarded as among the more indelible characters of black literature .
    6One reason , perhaps , that Harlem street life had such a powerful impact on Mrs. Petry , was that it was so far removed from her own genteel upbringing .
    7A native of Old Saybrook , where her father , Peter Lane , had opened a pharmacy in 1902 , she grew up in one of the few black families in the white resort .
    8Several members of her mother 's family were pharmacists , too , and in time Mrs. Petry graduated from the College of Pharmacy at the University of Connecticut and went to work at her father 's store .
    9But ever since a high school teacher who did not like her had praised her work and said she could become a writer , she had been writing stories and , as she later put it , gathering rejection slips .
    10Her life began to change in 1938 when she married George Petry , a Louisiana-born resident of Harlem she had met while both were visiting friends in Hartford .
    11The couple settled in Harlem , and Mrs. Petry held a variety of jobs , including writing advertising copy for The Amsterdam News and working as a reporter and editor of the women 's pages of The People 's Voice , a weekly started by Adam Clayton Powell Jr .
    12When The Crisis , a magazine of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People , paid her $ 20 for one of her stories , '' On Saturday , the Sirens at Noon , '' it came to the attention of an editor at Houghton-Mifflin and led to both a $ 2,500 literary award and a contract for what became '' The Street . ''
    13Mrs. Petry , who had written the book while her husband was in the service in World War II , was not quite prepared for the stir it created when it was published to both critical and popular acclaim .
    14But '' The Street , '' which sold 1.5 million copies , gave Mrs. Petry the wherewithal to retreat back to Old Saybrook , where she and her husband settled in a 200-year-old sea captain 's house and got an unlisted telephone number .
    15She followed '' The Street , '' with two other novels , '' Country Place , '' ( 1947 ) about about a serviceman who returns from the war to discover his wife is having an affair , and '' The Narrows , '' ( 1953 ) about an interracial affair .
    16Neither enjoyed the critical or commercial success of her first novel , however , and from then on Mrs. Petry , by then a mother , turned out books for children and young adults , among them two biographies , '' Tituba of Salem Village , '' and '' Harriet Tubman : Conductor on the Underground Railroad . ''
    17She also brought out a collection of her short stories , '' Legends of the Saints , '' but after its publication in 1970 , she limited her output to articles for college journals and the like .
    18Mrs. Petry had to endure a fresh burst of fame in 1992 when '' The Street , '' was reissued in paperback to a new round of acclaim .
    19She is survived by her husband and a daughter , Elisabeth , of Cromwell , Conn .
